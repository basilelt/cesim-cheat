File 1: Case Company DescriptionMobilé Inc. – Case DescriptionCesim Global ChallengeBackgroundThe US Department of Justice ordered the boards of directors of the major mobile handset manufacturers to dismiss and replace their top management due to years of cartel-like behaviour. Investigations found that the companies' directors had agreed amongst themselves in secret banquets on issues such as deliberately stagnating technology development plans, pricing the handsets artificially high, and other key matters that had set the companies in exactly the same financial and operational position.Finally, the board of directors has understood the serious nature of this situation and hired you to compete against the other restructured companies. The core operations of the companies operating in the mobile handset manufacturing industry consist of research and development, production and marketing. Sales revenues in the previous financial year were a bit more than 1 billion dollars. Due to collusion, profitability has been good, and the companies are also generating sufficient amounts of cash. This is a good starting point since it is likely that the increased competition and rapid technological evolution over the next years will tax both profitability and cash.History of the IndustryThe first international cellular mobile telephone network, NMT, was introduced in 1981. At the time, the transportable mobile phones were heavy and huge. Towards the end of the 80's actual hand portables came into the market and the era of the mobile phones industry began. Many of the mobile communications companies have evolved from multi-industry companies as a result of the recession in the end of the 80's and the beginning of the 90's, when the downward spin of the economy slowed down the general economic growth for a couple of years.Despite the economic slowdown, the communications business started to emerge from other business' shadow, and on July 1st, 1991, the world's first genuine GSM call was placed. GSM's American counterpart, CDMA, is the world's second most common mobile phone standard, covering a third of the world's mobile phone subscribers. The constant development of mobile phones in the 90's made the devices smaller and lighter, their technical design more complex, and increased the number of available functions.FOMA, the first 3G (third generation) network was launched in Japan in 2001 by NTT DoCoMo. The new technology made possible the transfer of voice data and non-voice data, paving the way for data-based mobile services and starting to transform the whole industry. By 2009 it had become clear that the overwhelming growth of data traffic could not be sustained with the existing technology, and industry started looking for new data-optimized technologies. The first 4G networks were launched by Sprint in the US and TeliaSonera in Scandinavia.Present DayCurrently, the companies have an annual sales revenue of about 1.2 billion USD, and due to relatively large R&D investments, the companies' handset businesses have developed favourably. Appealing and customer-driven designs have been named as some of the key success factors in the business.Markets: The companies sell their products in the U.S., Asia, and Europe.Production & R&D: Historically located in Atlanta (GA), but recently the companies have also started building plants in Asia. Europe was investigated for production, but complicated and inflexible labour laws would make operations difficult and expensive.Agreements: The companies actively negotiate agreements for subcontracting parts of their production and R&D to ensure flexibility.Future ChallengesThe pace of change in the industry has been rapid, and great expectations have been set for new technologies. The biggest challenge for a mobile handset manufacturer may be keeping up with technological evolution since R&D requires continuous large investments. It seems that not everything can be developed in-house and therefore partners for technology licensing are needed. Growth in global markets will probably create momentum for establishing more production facilities at least in Asia. As the industry evolves, co-operation among industry players has intensified. In recent years, the general public has become increasingly interested in ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance) matters; changes are imminent.OperationsProductionCharacteristic to high-tech companies, production is complicated and high costs are incurred in the beginning phases of the production of new models. Short product life-cycles force companies to adapt production processes rapidly.Learning Curve: As the company becomes more acquainted with a technology, production cost per unit will fall. Note that subcontracting production foregoes this learning curve effect.Subcontracting: Contract manufacturing adds useful extra capacity and can sometimes provide devices with significantly smaller costs.Environmental Factors: While technologies might be energy efficient for end-users, their complex production processes and rare metal requirements might fare worse environmentally. Improving production processes reduces environmental impact but involves investment costs and temporary capacity reductions.Sales and MarketingMarketing plays a significant role in promoting the brand and communicating to consumers. Marketing is particularly important in the U.S. and Europe, while its effect in Asia is less but still considerable. Typical marketing spending in the industry is 3-5% of Sales revenue.R&DConsumers continuously demand new products, and margins from old products decline rapidly. R&D can be performed in-house or outsourced by purchasing technology licenses.Process: Develop the base technology first, then add up to ten technology-specific features.Timing: In-house R&D benefits are available in the next period. Outsourced (licensed) R&D is available immediately.Costs: In-house R&D is an operating expense (not capitalised), costing lower over time compared to lump-sum licensing. A typical company spends 10% of Sales revenue on R&D.Human ResourcesInternal R&D is performed by a department initially consisting of 200 engineers.Turnover: High past turnover requires targeting a 10-12% rate going forward by managing salary levels, training, and utilization rates.Efficiency & ESG: Cumulative training and low attrition contribute to a more efficient workforce. Balances between personnel capacity, costs, efficiency, and public image (ESG) are critical.Technologies and FeaturesTechnologies: The companies currently manufacture Technology 1 handsets. New technologies require relatively large R&D investments. Technologies are network-dependent (e.g., a Tech 2 phone cannot operate on a Tech 1 network).Features: Product differentiation is achieved via features (design, cover, screen size, processor). You can add a minimum of 1 and a maximum of 10 features per technology.Transportation, Logistics, and TaxationLogistics Cost: Total logistics cost per unit = transportation cost + tariff. There is no cost for goods manufactured and sold in the same area.Environmental Impact: Intercontinental transport generates more CO2 emissions than domestic sales, though transportation emissions are insignificantly low compared to actual production.Transfer Pricing: R&D expenditure is allocated to plants globally (e.g., $10/12 \times 200$ mUSD for the US, $2/12 \times 200$ mUSD for Asia). Transfer prices are determined using multipliers (between 1 and 2) applied to the direct variable cost of production, which can be used to benefit from differing corporate tax rates.FinanceCompanies are listed on the stock exchange and can use equity financing (issuing shares). You can reward investors via dividends or share repurchases based on market valuation. Short-term debt is emergency funding with a premium over long-term loan rates. Internal loans can repatriate funds from Asia or Europe back to owners or finance plant investments.Market AreasUSA (Local Market): Leadership in wireless is unclear due to network technologies. Features are less appreciated than in Europe, but advertising responds well. Demand growth is steady at 5-10% p.a., potentially peaking at 15-20% p.a..Europe: High labour costs prevent production facilities here. Market growth is expected at 10% p.a. This market is the most responsive to product features.Asia: Highest growth potential (currently 20% p.a.). Consumers are less responsive to new technologies and features, but price responsiveness is higher than elsewhere.
