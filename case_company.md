# Case Company Description

## Mobile Inc. - Case Description

### Cesim Global Challenge

## Background

The US Department of Justice ordered the boards of directors of the major mobile handset manufacturers to dismiss and replace their top management due to years of cartel-like behavior. Investigations found that the companies' directors had agreed among themselves in secret banquets on issues such as deliberately stagnating technology development plans, pricing handsets artificially high, and other key matters that had set the companies in exactly the same financial and operational position.

Finally, the boards of directors understood the serious nature of this situation and hired you to compete against the other restructured companies. The core operations of companies in the mobile handset manufacturing industry consist of research and development, production, and marketing.

Sales revenues in the previous financial year were a bit more than 1 billion dollars. Due to collusion, profitability has been good, and the companies are also generating sufficient cash. This is a good starting point, since it is likely that increased competition and rapid technological evolution over the next years will tax both profitability and cash.

## History of the Industry

The first international cellular mobile telephone network, NMT, was introduced in 1981. At the time, transportable mobile phones were heavy and huge. Toward the end of the 1980s, actual hand portables came into the market and the era of the mobile phone industry began. Many mobile communications companies evolved from multi-industry companies as a result of the recession at the end of the 1980s and the beginning of the 1990s, when the downward spin of the economy slowed general economic growth for a couple of years.

Despite the economic slowdown, the communications business started to emerge from other businesses' shadow, and on July 1st, 1991, the world's first genuine GSM call was placed. GSM's American counterpart, CDMA, is the world's second most common mobile phone standard, covering a third of the world's mobile phone subscribers. Constant development of mobile phones in the 1990s made devices smaller and lighter, made technical design more complex, and increased the number of available functions.

FOMA, the first 3G (third generation) network, was launched in Japan in 2001 by NTT DoCoMo. The new technology made possible the transfer of voice and non-voice data, paving the way for data-based mobile services and starting to transform the whole industry. By 2009 it had become clear that the overwhelming growth of data traffic could not be sustained with existing technology, and the industry started looking for new data-optimized technologies. The first 4G networks were launched by Sprint in the US and TeliaSonera in Scandinavia.

## Present Day

Currently, the companies have annual sales revenue of about 1.2 billion USD, and due to relatively large R&D investments, the companies' handset businesses have developed favorably. Appealing and customer-driven designs have been named as key success factors in the business.

- Markets: The companies sell their products in the US, Asia, and Europe.
- Production and R&D: Historically located in Atlanta (GA), but recently companies have also started building plants in Asia. Europe was investigated for production, but complicated and inflexible labor laws would make operations difficult and expensive.
- Agreements: The companies actively negotiate agreements for subcontracting parts of production and R&D to ensure flexibility.

## Future Challenges

The pace of change in the industry has been rapid, and great expectations have been set for new technologies. The biggest challenge for a mobile handset manufacturer may be keeping up with technological evolution, since R&D requires continuous large investments. It seems that not everything can be developed in-house and therefore partners for technology licensing are needed.

Growth in global markets will probably create momentum for establishing more production facilities, at least in Asia. As the industry evolves, cooperation among industry players has intensified. In recent years, the general public has become increasingly interested in ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance) matters; changes are imminent.

## Operations

### Production

Characteristic of high-tech companies, production is complicated and high costs are incurred in the beginning phases of producing new models. Short product life cycles force companies to adapt production processes rapidly.

- Learning curve: As the company becomes more acquainted with a technology, production cost per unit falls. Note that subcontracting production forgoes this learning curve effect.
- Subcontracting: Contract manufacturing adds useful extra capacity and can sometimes provide devices at significantly lower costs.
- Environmental factors: While technologies might be energy efficient for end-users, complex production processes and rare metal requirements can fare worse environmentally. Improving production processes reduces environmental impact but involves investment costs and temporary capacity reductions.

### Sales and Marketing

Marketing plays a significant role in promoting the brand and communicating with consumers. Marketing is particularly important in the US and Europe, while its effect in Asia is lower but still considerable. Typical marketing spending in the industry is 3-5% of sales revenue.

### R&D

Consumers continuously demand new products, and margins from old products decline rapidly. R&D can be performed in-house or outsourced by purchasing technology licenses.

- Process: Develop the base technology first, then add up to ten technology-specific features.
- Timing: In-house R&D benefits are available in the next period. Outsourced (licensed) R&D is available immediately.
- Costs: In-house R&D is an operating expense (not capitalized), typically costing less over time compared to lump-sum licensing. A typical company spends 10% of sales revenue on R&D.

### Human Resources

Internal R&D is performed by a department initially consisting of 200 engineers.

- Turnover: High past turnover requires targeting a 10-12% rate going forward by managing salary levels, training, and utilization rates.
- Efficiency and ESG: Cumulative training and low attrition contribute to a more efficient workforce. Balancing personnel capacity, costs, efficiency, and public image (ESG) is critical.

### Technologies and Features

- Technologies: The companies currently manufacture Technology 1 handsets. New technologies require relatively large R&D investments. Technologies are network-dependent (for example, a Tech 2 phone cannot operate on a Tech 1 network).
- Features: Product differentiation is achieved through features (design, cover, screen size, processor). You can add a minimum of 1 and a maximum of 10 features per technology.

### Transportation, Logistics, and Taxation

- Logistics cost: Total logistics cost per unit = transportation cost + tariff. There is no cost for goods manufactured and sold in the same area.
- Environmental impact: Intercontinental transport generates more CO2 emissions than domestic sales, though transportation emissions are insignificantly low compared to actual production.
- Transfer pricing: R&D expenditure is allocated to plants globally (for example, $\frac{10}{12} \times 200$ mUSD for the US, $\frac{2}{12} \times 200$ mUSD for Asia). Transfer prices are determined using multipliers (between 1 and 2) applied to direct variable production cost, which can be used to benefit from differing corporate tax rates.

### Finance

Companies are listed on the stock exchange and can use equity financing (issuing shares). You can reward investors via dividends or share repurchases based on market valuation. Short-term debt is emergency funding with a premium over long-term loan rates. Internal loans can repatriate funds from Asia or Europe back to owners or finance plant investments.

## Market Areas

- USA (local market): Leadership in wireless is unclear due to network technologies. Features are less appreciated than in Europe, but advertising responds well. Demand growth is steady at 5-10% p.a., potentially peaking at 15-20% p.a.
- Europe: High labor costs prevent production facilities here. Market growth is expected at 10% p.a. This market is the most responsive to product features.
- Asia: Highest growth potential (currently 20% p.a.). Consumers are less responsive to new technologies and features, but price responsiveness is higher than elsewhere.
